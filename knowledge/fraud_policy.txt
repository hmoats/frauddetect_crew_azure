ACME FINANCIAL — FRAUD DETECTION POLICY & INVESTIGATION GUIDELINES
====================================================================
Document ID: FP-2025-003
Effective Date: 1 January 2025
Classification: Internal — Fraud Operations

1. RISK SCORE INTERPRETATION
-----------------------------
The machine-learning model outputs a probability score between 0.0 and 1.0.
Agents must apply the following thresholds when forming a verdict:

  - Score 0.00 – 0.30  →  LOW RISK. Approve unless other red flags are present.
  - Score 0.31 – 0.50  →  MODERATE RISK. Approve with a note; flag for
    periodic review if the customer has prior alerts.
  - Score 0.51 – 0.75  →  ELEVATED RISK. Default to FRAUD unless the analyst
    can document a clear legitimate explanation.
  - Score 0.76 – 1.00  →  HIGH RISK. Treat as FRAUD. Escalate to the senior
    fraud team for manual investigation within 24 hours.

An analyst may override the model's recommendation only when the transaction
context provides compelling evidence. Every override must include a written
justification referencing specific transaction attributes.

2. RED FLAG INDICATORS
-----------------------
The following patterns must always be called out in the explanation, whether
or not the model flagged them:

  a) Zero account balance at time of transaction.
  b) Transaction amount exceeding 80 % of the account balance.
  c) Unknown Merchant or merchant category marked "Unknown Merchant."
  d) Transaction occurring between 00:00 and 05:00 local time.
  e) Device type reported as "nan" or "Unknown Mobile Device."
  f) Mismatch between transaction location and customer home city/state.
  g) Multiple transactions from the same customer within a 5-minute window.
  h) Transaction description inconsistent with merchant category
     (e.g., "Bitcoin transaction" under "Clothing").

If two or more red flags co-occur, the verdict should be FRAUD regardless
of the model score.

3. VERDICT FORMAT REQUIREMENTS
-------------------------------
Every verdict returned to downstream systems must include:

  - customer_id        (string, must match the input)
  - verdict            (exactly "FRAUD" or "APPROVED", no other values)
  - risk_score         (float, 0.0–1.0, taken from the model output)
  - explanation        (2–3 sentences referencing real transaction attributes)
  - transaction_id     (string, from the record)
  - transaction_amount (float, from the record)
  - merchant_category  (string, from the record)
  - transaction_type   (string, from the record — Debit, Credit, etc.)
  - account_balance    (float, from the record)
  - city               (string, customer city)
  - device_type        (string, from the record)

Do NOT fabricate or assume any field value. If a field is missing from the
source record, use an empty string for text fields and 0.0 for numeric fields,
and note the missing data in the explanation.

4. ESCALATION RULES
---------------------
  - Any transaction above ₹100,000 with a risk score ≥ 0.51 must be
    flagged for manual review by the senior fraud team.
  - Any customer with three or more FRAUD verdicts in a 30-day window
    must trigger an account-level review.
  - Transactions involving cryptocurrency keywords ("Bitcoin", "crypto",
    "NFT", "wallet top-up") require additional scrutiny regardless of
    the model score.

5. DATA HANDLING & PRIVACY
----------------------------
  - Never include raw PII (full name, email, phone number) in the
    explanation field. Reference the customer by customer_id only.
  - Transaction records must not be stored beyond the analysis session
    unless required for regulatory audit.
  - All verdict outputs are classified as Confidential and must be
    transmitted over encrypted channels only.

6. MODEL LIMITATIONS & ANALYST RESPONSIBILITY
-----------------------------------------------
The pre-trained model was built on historical transaction data and may not
account for:
  - Emerging fraud patterns not present in the training set.
  - Regional spending patterns that differ from the training distribution.
  - Seasonal spikes (e.g., festival-season purchases) that may resemble
    anomalous behaviour.

Analysts are expected to apply professional judgment. The model is a
decision-support tool, not a decision-maker. The analyst's verdict is
the authoritative output.
